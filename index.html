1. Computaion of some variance

Let $X', Y'$ be random variables such that \[X'=X(1+\sigma\epsilon_1),\quad Y'=Y(1+\sigma\rho\epsilon_1+\sigma\gamma\epsilon_2)\] where $X,Y,\sigma, \rho,\gamma$ are constants, and $\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2$ are independent random variables which follow standard normal distribution, i.e. \[\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2\stackrel{i.i.d.}{\sim} N(0,1)\]

Our aim is to calculate the variance of $X'Y'$ and we will see why we do so. The following stuff will assist us to this job.

Definition 1.1

Given two random variables $X$ and $Y$, the covariance between $X$ and $Y$ is defined as \[\cov(X,Y)=E[(X-E(X))(Y-E(Y))]=E(XY)-E(X)E(Y)\]

Proposition 1.2

  1. $\cov(X,X)=\var(X)$
  2. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then $\cov(X,Y)=0$
  3. $\cov(X,Y)=\cov(Y,X)$
  4. $\cov(aX,Y)=a\cov(X,Y)$ if $a$ is a constant.
  5. $\cov(X+c,Y)=\cov(X,Y)$ if $c$ is a constant.
  6. $\cov(X+Y,Z)=\cov(X,Z)+\cov(Y,Z)$
  7. Generally, \[\cov\left (\sum_{i=1}^m a_iX_i, \sum_{j=1}^n b_jY_j\right )=\sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n a_ib_j\cov(X_i,Y_j)\]

Proposition 1.3

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent random variables, then $f(X)$ and $g(Y)$ are independent for any functions $f$ and $g$.

Example 1.4

Therefore,

  1. $\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1)=\var(\epsilon_1)=1$
  2. $\cov(\epsilon_1^2,\epsilon_1^2)=\var(\epsilon_1^2)=2$
  3. $\cov(\epsilon_1\epsilon_2,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)=\var(\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)=1$
  4. $\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2)=0$, $\cov(\epsilon_2,\epsilon_1^2)=0$
  5. Since $E(\epsilon_1^3)=0$, \[\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1^2)=E(\epsilon_1^3)-E(\epsilon_1)E(\epsilon_1^2)=0-0=0\]
  6. Since $\epsilon_1^2$ and $\epsilon_2$ are independent, \[\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)=E(\epsilon_1^2\epsilon_2)-E(\epsilon_1)E(\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)=E(\epsilon_1^2)E(\epsilon_2)-E(\epsilon_1)E(\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)=0-0=0\]

Example 1.5

\[\var(X'Y')=X^2Y^2[\sigma^2(\rho+1)^2+\sigma^2\gamma^2+2\sigma^4\rho^2+\sigma^4\gamma^2]\]

Proof:

\begin{align*} \var(X'Y')&=\cov(X'Y',X'Y')\\ &= \cov(XY(1+\sigma(\rho+1)\epsilon_1+\sigma\gamma\epsilon_2+\sigma^2\rho\epsilon_1^2+\sigma^2\gamma\epsilon_1\epsilon_2),\\ &\quad\quad XY(1+\sigma(\rho+1)\epsilon_1+\sigma\gamma\epsilon_2+\sigma^2\rho\epsilon_1^2+\sigma^2\gamma\epsilon_1\epsilon_2))\\ &=X^2Y^2\cov(\sigma(\rho+1)\epsilon_1+\sigma\gamma\epsilon_2+\sigma^2\rho\epsilon_1^2+\sigma^2\gamma\epsilon_1\epsilon_2,\\ &\quad\quad \sigma(\rho+1)\epsilon_1+\sigma\gamma\epsilon_2+\sigma^2\rho\epsilon_1^2+\sigma^2\gamma\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)\\ &=X^2Y^2[\sigma^2(\rho+1)^2\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1)+\sigma^2\gamma^2\cov(\epsilon_2,\epsilon_2)+\sigma^4\rho^2\cov(\epsilon_1^2,\epsilon_1^2)\\ &\quad\quad+\sigma^4\gamma^2\cov(\epsilon_1\epsilon_2,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)+2\sigma^2(\rho+1)\gamma\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2)+2\sigma^3(\rho+1)\rho\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1^2)\\ &\quad\quad +2\sigma^3(\rho+1)\gamma\cov(\epsilon_1,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)+2\sigma^3\gamma\rho\cov(\epsilon_2,\epsilon_1^2)+2\sigma^3\gamma^2\cov(\epsilon_2,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)\\ &\quad\quad +2\sigma^4\rho\gamma\cov(\epsilon_1^2,\epsilon_1\epsilon_2)]\\ &=X^2Y^2[\sigma^2(\rho+1)^2+\sigma^2\gamma^2+2\sigma^4\rho^2+\sigma^4\gamma^2+0+0+0+0+0+0]\\ &=X^2Y^2[\sigma^2(\rho+1)^2+\sigma^2\gamma^2+2\sigma^4\rho^2+\sigma^4\gamma^2] \end{align*}

Example 0.6

In real life application, for example one can let $X$ be the initial value of EUR to CNY exchange rate (i.e. the number of CNY for 1 EUR) and Y be the initital value of CNY to USD exchange rate, hence our random variable $X'Y'$ will be the exchange rate of EUR to USD. In this case, $\rho$ is the correlation coefficient between these two exhcange rates, while having a constraint that $\rho^2+\gamma^2=1$. So now we can calculate the variance of EUR to USD exchange rate.

Let $X=8.4, Y=1/7, \sigma=0.15, \rho=0.6, \gamma=\sqrt{1-\rho^2}=0.8$, then \[\var(X'Y')=0.10467144\]
